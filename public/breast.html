Breast Self-Examination Guide

Learn the proper technique for monthly breast self-exams to detect changes early

1

Preparation

Examine your breasts monthly, 7-10 days after your period starts. Use the same time each month.

  • Stand in front of a mirror
  • Remove your top and bra
  • Place hands on hips
2

Visual Inspection

Look for changes in size, shape, skin texture, or nipple position.

  • Check both breasts
  • Look for dimpling or puckering
  • Note any nipple discharge
3

Physical Examination

Use your fingertips to feel for lumps or changes in breast tissue.

  • Use light, medium, and firm pressure
  • Examine in circular motions
  • Cover entire breast and armpit area

Understanding Breast Health

Knowledge is power. Learn about breast anatomy, common conditions, and preventive care

Breast Anatomy

Understanding the structure of your breasts helps you recognize normal changes and potential concerns.

  • Glandular tissue: Milk-producing glands
  • Fatty tissue: Gives breasts their shape
  • Connective tissue: Provides support
  • Lymph nodes: Part of the immune system

Hormonal Changes

Breasts change throughout your life due to hormonal fluctuations.

  • Menstruation: Monthly breast tenderness
  • Pregnancy: Growth and milk production
  • Menopause: Decreased glandular tissue
  • Hormone therapy: Can affect breast density

Risk Factors

Some factors increase breast cancer risk, but most women who develop breast cancer have no known risk factors.

  • Age: Risk increases with age
  • Family history: Genetic factors
  • Lifestyle: Diet, exercise, alcohol
  • Reproductive history: Age at first pregnancy

Screening Guidelines

Regular screening is crucial for early detection.

  • Mammogram: Annual screening starting at 40
  • Clinical exam: Annual professional examination
  • Self-exam: Monthly breast self-examination
  • MRI: For high-risk individuals

Common Breast Concerns

Most breast changes are benign, but it's important to know what to look for

Fibrocystic Changes

Common benign condition causing lumpy, tender breasts. Often related to hormonal changes.

Lumpiness Tenderness Cyclic pain

Fibroadenomas

Solid, benign tumors that are common in young women. Usually painless and moveable.

Firm lump Moveable Rubber-like texture

Cysts

Fluid-filled sacs that can cause pain and tenderness. Often resolve on their own.

Round lump Tender to touch Changes with cycle

When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention for these changes:

  • New lump or mass
  • Nipple discharge
  • Skin changes or dimpling
  • Persistent pain
  • Changes in breast shape

Breast Health Lifestyle

Simple lifestyle choices that support breast health and overall well-being

Nutrition

Eat a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. Maintain a healthy weight.

Exercise

Aim for 150 minutes of moderate exercise weekly. Physical activity helps maintain healthy hormone levels.

Limit Alcohol

Limit alcohol consumption to reduce breast cancer risk. Consider avoiding alcohol altogether.

Avoid Smoking

Quit smoking to improve overall health and reduce cancer risk throughout the body.

Family Planning

Consider breastfeeding if possible. It may offer protective benefits for breast health.

Stress Management

Practice stress-reduction techniques. Chronic stress may impact hormone balance.
